#### 1. The fundamental difference between the two models

Before comparing, you need to understand where these two models actually differ - not in their labels, but in how you work day to day.

| | **Outsource** | **Product Company** |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer** | External (foreign or domestic clients) | Internal (the company's own product) |
| **Project cycle** | Short to medium-term (3–18 months per project) | Long-term, ongoing |
| **Tech stack** | Changes with each project | Relatively fixed, goes deeper |
| **Ownership** | Low - you build to client specs | High - you make more decisions |
| **Learning speed** | Fast and broad | Deep in one direction |
| **Deadline pressure** | High, rigid | Moderate, more flexible |
| **Fresher-friendly?** | Yes - headcount-driven hiring | Less so - more selective |

Outsource is not "worse" than product. These are two different paths suited to two different types of people and two different stages of a career.

#### 3. How the career paths actually differ

#### 3.1 Outsource - breadth first, depth later

In the first two years at an outsource company, you can touch 3–5 different tech stacks, work with diverse client industries, and build a broad CV. The upside: you're not locked into one technology from day one.

The downside: if you don't actively go deep on your own time, you risk becoming someone who knows a lot but masters nothing. Four years of outsource with nothing to show beyond task execution makes the jump to senior much harder.

**Realistic career path:** Fresher → Junior → Senior Developer (3–5 years) → Tech Lead / BA / PM depending on direction. Many developers move from outsource to a product company at mid-level after 3–4 years - this is a common and effective strategy.

#### 3.2 Product company - depth, ownership, and systems thinking

Working in product forces you to understand the system at a deeper level: why the architecture was designed this way, what technical decisions were made last year and for what reason. You have context. You have ownership.

The upside: faster growth in technical thinking, a clear understanding of the product lifecycle, and a stronger path to becoming a senior with real depth. The downside: if the company is small and the product doesn't scale, your technical toolkit stays limited.

**Realistic career path:** Junior → Mid → Senior → Staff Engineer or Tech Lead. Faster progression when the product is in active growth.

#### 4. When you should choose outsource

Choose outsource if any of the following apply:

**You need a job quickly and need income now.** Outsource companies hire more freshers and set a lower bar. If you're financially on your own, this is the practical choice.

**You want to learn multiple technologies fast.** One or two solid years at a good outsource company means touching React, Node, Docker, CI/CD, REST APIs - all at once. That breadth is hard to get when you're tied to a single product from the start.

**You don't know which direction you want to go yet.** Frontend? Backend? DevOps? Outsource gives you room to explore before committing to depth.

**You received a strong outsource offer over a weak product offer.** Not every product company is worth the label. A professional outsource firm with complex projects, good mentors, and a clear process beats a chaotic product startup where no one teaches you anything.

If you're curious what a real outsource junior interview looks like, read [My Junior Outsource IT Interview: What They Really Asked](/en/phong-van-outsource-junior) for a concrete picture before you decide.

#### 5. When you should choose a product company

Choose product if:

**You already have a solid technical foundation (6–12 months of self-study or internship).** You won't be overwhelmed, and you'll actually take advantage of the environment.

**You want to build ownership early.** If you want the feeling of "I built this," product is the right place. Outsource rarely gives you that.

**The product is genuinely growing.** Working on a product in a real growth phase is an extremely valuable experience - you learn to scale systems, handle real traffic, and make technical decisions that affect actual users.

**You have a clear goal of reaching senior or staff level within 3–4 years.** A product company with good mentors will accelerate you considerably.

#### 6. 5 mistakes freshers make when choosing

**Mistake 1: Romanticizing product companies without checking the reality**
Not every product company is Grab or Shopee. Many "product" startups are actually building an internal admin tool no one uses, with an old tech stack and no mentor in sight. Ask hard questions about the product, DAU, and the engineering team before accepting.

*Fix:* Ask directly in the interview: "How many active users does the product have? How large is the backend team? What framework does the codebase use and since when?"

**Mistake 2: Fearing outsource based on secondhand opinions**
"You don't learn anything at outsource companies" is simply wrong. It depends on the company, the project, and your attitude. Some of the strongest senior developers in the market came up through outsource.

*Fix:* Evaluate the quality of the project and team - not the outsource vs. product label.

**Mistake 3: Choosing based on what your friends are doing**
"My friend just joined a product company, so I need to as well." Your situation is not their situation.

*Fix:* Ask yourself three honest questions: What do I need most right now - income, breadth of experience, or depth? Where am I technically right now? Which offer is genuinely better when I look at the team and the work?

**Mistake 4: Ignoring the people and the mentorship**
A senior developer who will actively mentor you is worth more than any company label. Outsource with great mentorship beats a product company where no one pays attention to you.

*Fix:* Ask about the onboarding process, team structure, and who will be reviewing your code.

**Mistake 5: Treating your first job as a permanent decision**
Many freshers overthink this as if choosing wrong now will derail their entire career. It won't. This is your first job - not a lifelong commitment.

*Fix:* Set specific 12–18 month goals, reassess at that point, and be ready to change direction if needed. See [5 Red Flags in a Job Offer You Should Never Ignore](/tu-choi-offer-it) for signs that a company isn't the right fit.

#### 7. FAQ

**Which pays more for freshers - outsource or product company?**
Product companies pay 20–35% more on average, but the hiring bar is also higher. Freshers without a strong enough foundation may not pass a product company's technical interview. Compare actual offers - don't compare labels.

**How do outsource and product companies differ in tech stack?**
Outsource typically rotates stacks across projects - you could touch 3–5 different technologies in two years. Product companies go deep on a fixed stack, but usually invest more in internal tooling and tend to run newer tech since they're maintaining a product long-term. Want breadth → outsource. Want depth in one direction → product.

**Is it too late to switch to product after 1–2 years in outsource?**
Not at all - and this is actually the most common path. A CV with 1–2 years of real-world outsource experience across multiple stacks is an asset when applying to product companies at junior or mid level.

**Can a fresher with zero experience get into a product company?**
Yes, but you need to prepare more carefully: a quality personal portfolio project, a basic grasp of system design, and solid command of one tech stack rather than shallow knowledge of many. The technical bar at product companies is roughly 30–50% higher than at outsource - but if your foundation is solid, it's absolutely achievable.

**Are product companies in Vietnam actually better than outsource companies?**
It depends entirely on the company. MoMo, VNG, Sky Mavis, KiotViet - yes. An anonymous 5-person startup building an internal reporting tool - not necessarily. Research before you romanticize the label.

**What should I say if an interviewer asks "do you prefer outsource or product?"**
Don't say "I prefer product" if you're interviewing for an outsource company. The mature answer: "I want an environment where I can learn a lot, work on real projects, and have a clear path to grow - and from what I've seen, this company offers exactly that." Focus on the value of the place you're interviewing with.

**Do outsource companies offer remote work?**
More and more outsource companies offer hybrid or fully remote options, especially on overseas projects. This is a significant advantage compared to product companies that require full on-site.

## Summary

Outsource or product - there's no universally right answer. The right answer is the one that fits where you are right now:

- **Need a job quickly, want breadth, not yet ready for product:** → Outsource first
- **Have a solid foundation, want depth and ownership, got a good product offer:** → Go product now
- **Currently in outsource and want to switch:** → 18–24 months of real-world experience is enough to apply at product companies at mid level

What matters more than the label: is the team strong, will someone mentor you, is the work actually building something real?
