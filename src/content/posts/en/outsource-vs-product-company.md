#### 1. What actually separates these two models

Before comparing, you need an accurate picture of what these models actually mean day-to-day - not just the label.

| | **Outsource** | **Product Company** |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer** | External (foreign or local clients) | Internal (the company's own product) |
| **Project cycle** | Short to mid-term (3–18 months per project) | Long-term, continuous |
| **Tech stack** | Changes per project | Relatively fixed, goes deep |
| **Ownership** | Low - you implement client requirements | High - you influence decisions |
| **Speed of learning** | Wide breadth quickly | Deep expertise in one direction |
| **Deadline pressure** | High, rigid | Moderate, more flexible |
| **Fresher hiring** | Relatively easy - headcount-driven | Harder - selective hiring |

Outsource is not inherently worse than product - these are two different paths, suited for different people and different career stages.

**The honest read:** Product companies pay roughly 20–35% more on average - but the hiring bar is also higher. Many freshers don't qualify for product company technical interviews right out of university. Outsource is the more accessible entry point to get real-world experience in year one.

#### 3. How career paths diverge

#### 3.1. Outsource - breadth first, depth later

In your first two years at a decent outsource company, you might touch 3–5 different tech stacks, work across multiple industries, and understand how different clients solve different problems. Strength: a wide CV, exposure to many tools and domains.

Weakness: without intentional deep-learning outside work hours, you can become someone who knows many things but masters none. After 3–4 years of outsource work without notable depth, the jump to senior is harder.

**Typical path:** Fresher → Junior → Senior Developer (3–5 years) → Tech Lead/BA/PM depending on direction. Many outsource engineers switch to product companies at mid-level after 3–4 years with a diverse CV - this is a common and effective strategy.

#### 3.2. Product company - depth, ownership, and systems thinking

Working on a product means understanding the system at a deeper layer: why the architecture was designed this way, what technical decisions were made last year and why. You have context. You have ownership.

Strength: faster growth in technical thinking, clear understanding of the product lifecycle, easier path to becoming a senior with real depth. Weakness: if the company is small and the product isn't scaling, your technical exposure stays narrow.

**Typical path:** Junior → Mid → Senior → Staff Engineer or Tech Lead. Growth accelerates significantly when the product is in active growth mode.

#### 4. When to choose outsource

Choose outsource if you fall into one of these situations:

**You need income quickly and reliably:** Outsource companies hire more freshers and have a lower entry bar. If you need to support yourself immediately, this is the realistic choice.

**You want to learn many technologies fast:** The first 1–2 years at a good outsource company can expose you to React, Node.js, Docker, CI/CD, REST APIs - multiple things in parallel. Hard to replicate if you're locked into one product from day one.

**You're not sure which direction suits you yet:** Frontend? Backend? DevOps? Outsource gives you more surface area to find out before specializing.

**You received a better outsource offer than a weak product offer:** Not every product company is good. A professional outsource firm with complex projects, strong mentors, and clear processes beats a chaotic product startup with no one to learn from.

#### 5. When to choose a product company

Choose product if:

**You already have a solid technical foundation (6–12 months of self-study or internship):** You won't be overwhelmed and you'll actually extract value from the environment.

**You want ownership from the start:** If you care about the feeling of "I built this," product is the right place. Outsource rarely gives you that sense.

**The product is genuinely growing:** Working on a product in active growth is a high-value experience - you learn real scaling challenges, handle real traffic, and make technical decisions that affect real users.

**You have a clear goal of reaching senior/staff level within 3–4 years:** A product company with good mentorship will accelerate your growth significantly faster.

#### 6. 5 mistakes juniors make when choosing

**Mistake 1: Romanticizing product companies without checking reality**
Not every product company is a high-growth tech firm. Many "product" startups are building internal tools with no users, outdated tech stacks, and zero mentorship culture. The label alone means nothing.

*Fix:* Ask directly in interviews: "How many active users does the product have? How many engineers on the backend team? What framework and what vintage is the codebase?"

**Mistake 2: Fearing outsource based on secondhand opinions**
"You don't learn anything at outsource" is wrong. It depends on the company, the project, and your own attitude. Many of the best senior developers in the market came from outsource backgrounds.

*Fix:* Evaluate project quality and team quality - not the company label.

**Mistake 3: Following your peers instead of your own situation**
"My friend joined a product company, so I have to too." Your circumstances are different from your friend's.

*Fix:* Ask yourself three questions: What do I need most right now - income, broad exposure, or depth? Where am I technically? Which offer is actually stronger when I look at team and project quality?

**Mistake 4: Ignoring the human factor and mentorship**
A skilled senior developer who's willing to mentor you is worth more than the company's label. Outsource with a great mentor beats a product company where nobody cares about your development.

*Fix:* Ask about the onboarding process, team structure, and who will review your code.

**Mistake 5: Treating the first decision like it's permanent**
Many freshers agonize over this choice as if it will define their entire career. It won't. This is your first job, not a lifetime commitment.

*Fix:* Set a 12–18 month goal, reassess after that, and be willing to change direction. See [5 Red Flags in a Job Offer](/en/tu-choi-offer-it) for signs a company isn't the right fit early on.

#### 7. FAQ

**Which pays more for freshers - outsource or product?**
Product companies pay roughly 20–35% more on average, but the hiring bar is also higher. Freshers who can't clear a product company's technical interview end up with no offer at all. Always compare specific offers rather than labels.

**Is it too late to switch from outsource to product after 1–2 years?**
Not at all - and this is actually a common path. A CV with 1–2 years of hands-on outsource experience across multiple tech stacks is often an asset when applying to product companies at junior or mid-level.

**Are product companies in Vietnam actually better than outsource?**
Depends entirely on the company. Some established Vietnamese product firms offer genuinely excellent environments. Unknown startups with five people building an internal admin panel - not necessarily. Research thoroughly before committing to the label.

**If the interviewer asks "do you prefer outsource or product?" what should I say?**
Don't say "I prefer product" if you're interviewing at an outsource company. A mature answer: "I want an environment where I can keep learning and work on real problems - I think what your company is offering fits that well." Focus on the value of where you're actually interviewing.

**Do outsource companies offer remote work?**
Increasingly yes. Many outsource firms - especially those serving foreign clients - offer hybrid or full remote. This is a meaningful advantage worth factoring in when comparing offers.

## Summary

Outsource or product - no universally correct answer. The correct answer depends on where you are right now:

- **Need a job fast, want broad exposure, don't yet qualify for product:** → Start with outsource
- **Solid foundation, want depth and ownership, received a strong product offer:** → Go product directly
- **Currently at outsource and want to switch:** → 18–24 months of solid hands-on experience is enough to apply for mid-level product roles

What matters more than the label: is the team strong, will someone mentor you, and will the project actually build something real?
