Landing your first IT job is exciting - but the interview stage can feel overwhelming if you don't know what to expect. Most tech companies follow a predictable pattern when screening fresh graduates or entry-level candidates. This guide walks you through the 10 most common IT fresher interview questions, explains what interviewers are really testing, and shows you how to answer with confidence.

Whether you're applying for a developer, QA, or IT support role, these questions show up again and again at startups and large enterprises alike.

#### 1\. "Tell me about yourself."

**What they're testing:** Your communication skills, self-awareness, and how well you can summarize your background in a structured way.

**How to answer:** Use the Present-Past-Future formula. Start with your current status ("I just graduated with a degree in Computer Science"), mention relevant past projects briefly, then explain what you're looking for next.

**Example answer:** "I recently graduated from \[University\] with a degree in Software Engineering. During my studies, I built a web-based inventory system using Laravel and Vue.js as my thesis project. I'm now looking for a backend developer role where I can grow my skills in a real production environment."

**Pro tip:** Keep it under 2 minutes. Avoid reciting your entire CV - focus on what's most relevant to the role you're applying for.

#### 2\. "Why did you choose IT as your career?"

**What they're testing:** Genuine motivation and passion. Interviewers want to know you chose this field intentionally - not just because it pays well.

**How to answer:** Be honest and specific. Reference a real experience that sparked your interest - a project, a course, a problem you solved. Then connect that story to your career goals.

**Pro tip:** Avoid generic answers like "I love computers." Go deeper - talk about a specific moment where you built or fixed something and realized this was the right path for you.

#### 3\. "What programming languages do you know?"

**What they're testing:** Technical breadth and honesty about your skill level. Companies don't expect freshers to know everything - they want to see that you know what you're good at versus what you're still learning.

**How to answer:** Group your languages by proficiency. For example: "I'm most comfortable with JavaScript and PHP, which I've used in multiple projects. I also have working knowledge of Python from coursework, and I'm currently exploring Go on the side."

**Pro tip:** Never exaggerate. If you list a language, be ready for follow-up questions. Saying "I've done tutorials and small exercises in X" is far better than claiming full proficiency and getting stuck on a basic question.

#### 4\. "Explain Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) concepts."

**What they're testing:** Foundational software engineering knowledge. This is one of the most universal technical questions across all IT roles.

**The four pillars of OOP you must know:**

*   **Encapsulation:** Bundling data and methods inside a class, and restricting direct access from outside using access modifiers (private/public).
*   **Inheritance:** A child class can inherit properties and methods from a parent class, promoting code reuse.
*   **Polymorphism:** The same method name can behave differently in different contexts - through method overriding or overloading.
*   **Abstraction:** Hiding implementation details and exposing only the necessary interface to the user.

**Pro tip:** Give a real-world analogy for each concept. A car is a great example of abstraction - you use the steering wheel and pedals without needing to understand the internal mechanics of the engine.

#### 5\. "What is the difference between a Stack and a Queue?"

**What they're testing:** Basic data structure knowledge, which is fundamental to problem-solving and coding interviews.

**Stack:** A Last-In, First-Out (LIFO) structure. Think of a stack of plates - you always add and remove from the top. Real-world uses include undo/redo functions, function call stacks, and browser navigation history.

**Queue:** A First-In, First-Out (FIFO) structure. Think of a line at a ticket counter - the first person in is the first served. Real-world uses include task scheduling, print queues, and message queues in distributed systems.

**Pro tip:** Interviewers love it when you connect abstract CS concepts to real-world use cases. Mention where you've seen these structures used in actual code or frameworks.

#### 6\. "What is a REST API? How does it work?"

**What they're testing:** Understanding of how modern web applications communicate - a must-know for any web or mobile developer role.

**How to answer:** REST (Representational State Transfer) is an architectural style for designing networked applications. A REST API allows two systems to communicate over HTTP using standard methods: GET (retrieve), POST (create), PUT/PATCH (update), and DELETE (remove). Responses are typically in JSON format.

**Key principles to mention:** Stateless requests (each request contains all needed information), resource-based URLs (e.g., /users/123), and standard HTTP status codes (200, 201, 404, 500).

**Pro tip:** Mention a project where you built or consumed a REST API. Even a simple school project demonstrates practical understanding, which is more impressive than textbook knowledge alone.

#### 7\. "How does Git work? Walk me through your typical workflow."

**What they're testing:** Practical version control skills. No modern development team works without Git - interviewers want to confirm you can function in a collaborative environment.

**How to answer:** Explain the basic Git flow - initialize a repo, create a feature branch, commit changes, push to remote, and open a pull request for code review. Mention commands you actually use: **git clone, git checkout -b, git add, git commit, git push, git merge, git pull**.

Also cover: branching strategy (feature branches vs. main/develop), how you handle merge conflicts, and the purpose of pull requests in team workflows.

**Pro tip:** Mention your GitHub or GitLab profile if you have public projects. Showing real commit history is far more convincing than just describing your workflow in words.

#### 8\. "What is your biggest weakness?"

**What they're testing:** Self-awareness and emotional maturity. This is a behavioral question - they're not looking for a flaw that disqualifies you. They want to see whether you can reflect honestly and show a growth mindset.

**How to answer:** Choose a real weakness that is not critical to the core responsibilities of the job. Then - crucially - show what you're actively doing to improve it.

**Example answer:** "I used to struggle with estimating how long a task would take, which sometimes caused delays. I've been addressing this by breaking tasks into smaller subtasks, tracking actual vs. estimated time, and using Trello to visualize my workload. I've improved significantly, but I know there's still room to grow."

**Pro tip:** Never say "I'm a perfectionist." It's overused and sounds dishonest. Interviewers have heard it thousands of times and it tells them nothing real about you.

#### 9\. "Describe a project you're proud of."

**What they're testing:** Technical problem-solving ability, initiative, and your capacity to explain technical work clearly to others. This is your opportunity to stand out.

**How to answer:** Use the STAR method - Situation (context), Task (your role), Action (what you did), Result (outcome). Be specific about technologies used, challenges you overcame, and what you learned from the experience.

**Example answer:** "During my final year, I built an online booking system for a local clinic as my thesis. The main challenge was handling concurrent appointments without double-booking. I solved it using row-level locking in MySQL. The system handled around 200 active users during our testing phase. I'm particularly proud of the clean architecture - my supervisor used it as a best-practice example for other students."

**Pro tip:** Prepare 2-3 projects before the interview. Different interviewers may ask follow-up questions in very different directions, so having variety gives you flexibility.

#### 10\. "Where do you see yourself in 3 years?"

**What they're testing:** Ambition, career awareness, and whether your goals align with what the company can offer. They also want to gauge whether you're likely to commit for a reasonable period or leave after 6 months.

**How to answer:** Be honest but strategic. Show ambition without sounding like you're planning to job-hop. Connect your growth goals to the role and company you're interviewing for.

**Example answer:** "In 3 years, I'd like to grow from a junior developer who contributes to individual features into someone who can own entire modules or small products. I want to deepen my backend expertise - ideally in high-traffic systems - and eventually take on some mentoring responsibility for newer team members. I see this role as a strong foundation for that path."

**Pro tip:** Research the company's career progression before your interview. Referencing a specific growth track you read about in their engineering blog or job page shows genuine preparation and interest.

### Final Thoughts

No one expects a fresher to have all the answers - but interviewers do expect you to be prepared, self-aware, and eager to learn. Master these 10 questions, practice your answers out loud, and walk in with confidence. The best candidates aren't always the ones with the most knowledge - they're the ones who communicate clearly and show genuine enthusiasm for the craft.
