## Why freshers fear rejecting an offer

The most common mindset: *"I have no experience yet - if I turn this down, what else do I have to choose from?"*

That fear is understandable, but it causes many juniors to accept the wrong offer and job-hop after 3–6 months - which actually does more damage to your CV than simply not taking the offer in the first place.

The truth is: a bad offer isn't a "stepping stone." It's **wasted time** in an environment that won't help you grow. Six months at the wrong company is worse than six months continuing to look for the right one.

## Before and after learning to spot red flags

**Before:** Accept an offer out of fear there won't be another → two months in you can feel something's off → you endure four more months → you leave with six months of vague, unmemorable experience on your CV.

**After:** You spot 2–3 red flags during the interview process → you ask direct questions → you decline the offer with solid reasoning → you keep applying and land a better fit within 3–4 weeks.

The gap between these two scenarios isn't luck - it's the skill of evaluating offers.

## 5 red flags you should never ignore

### Salary below market with no explanation

Compare with [entry-level and junior IT salary benchmarks for 2026](/entry-level-software-engineer-salary-2026): if the offer comes in 20–30% below market and HR only says things like *"the company is in an early stage"* or *"we'll review after probation"* with no concrete numbers, that's a serious warning sign. According to the [Vietnam IT Market Report by TopDev](https://topdev.vn/) (for Vietnam market reference), fresher IT salaries in Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi typically range from 8–15 million VND (~$320–$600 USD) depending on the tech stack - use this as your baseline.

Ask directly: *"What percentage raise do people typically get after probation? Can you show me the salary band for this role?"*

### Chaotic or disrespectful interview process

How a company treats candidates during hiring usually reflects how they treat employees once you're in. Watch out for:

- Cancelling or rescheduling interviews multiple times without notice
- Interviewers who clearly haven't read your CV before the call
- No feedback or result within the promised timeframe
- Interview questions that have nothing to do with the actual job

### Vague or shifting job description

If the job description says "ReactJS Developer" but when you dig deeper HR says "you might also handle some WordPress and SEO as needed" - that's scope creep before you've even started. Juniors often get hired with a developer title but end up doing everything except actual technical work.

Ask directly: *"In the first three months, what will I be spending 80% of my time on, specifically?"*

### No mentor or onboarding plan

For freshers and juniors, the learning environment matters just as much as the salary. If you ask about onboarding and get answers like *"you'll learn by picking things up as you go"* or *"a senior will help you out when they have time"* - there's a good chance you'll be thrown in the deep end with no real support.

Ask: *"Does the team do code reviews? How many hours per week does a junior typically pair program with a senior?"*

### Pressure to sign immediately

*"We need your decision by end of today"* is one of the clearest red flags there is. Good companies understand that candidates need time to think. Pressure to sign on the spot usually comes with contract clauses they don't want you to read carefully - early-exit penalties, unpaid overtime, or overly broad non-disclosure terms.

Always ask for at least 48–72 hours to review the contract, no matter how much pressure you're under.

## How to evaluate an offer before deciding

Run through this three-group checklist before making your decision:

**Group 1 – Financials:** Is the salary enough to live on? Are there KPIs tied to your pay? What are the actual bonus, social insurance, and allowance figures? See [how to negotiate your first IT salary](/junior-developer-salary-negotiation) if you want to negotiate before outright rejecting.

**Group 2 – Growth:** Is there a senior you can actually learn from? Does the tech stack align with where you want to go? What does your CV look like after one year here?

**Group 3 – Culture:** What are the real working hours? Is overtime compensated? What do Glassdoor or ITviec reviews say about this company?

If both Group 2 and Group 3 are red, no salary amount makes up for it.

## Real case study

**Situation:** Minh, a React fresher, received an offer from a web agency in Ho Chi Minh City - 8 million VND/month (~$320 USD). The job description listed ReactJS, but after asking around, 70% of the actual work was WordPress projects for clients. React was only used in 1–2 projects per year. There was no senior frontend developer on the team.

**Decision:** Minh turned down the offer after running through the growth checklist. He kept applying for three more weeks and landed a product company offer at 9.5 million VND/month (~$380 USD) with a full React stack and a senior lead who reviewed code every day.

**Six months later:** Minh could build features independently, had real project work to show on his CV, while a classmate who took the agency offer was still stuck doing WordPress maintenance.

## 5 common mistakes when evaluating offers

**Mistake 1 – Comparing raw salary numbers instead of growth potential:** 8M VND at a product company with mentorship is usually better than 10M VND at an agency where you learn nothing.

**Fix:** Before comparing numbers, ask yourself: *"What will I be better at after one year here?"*

**Mistake 2 – Trusting verbal promises:** *"We'll raise your salary after probation"*, *"There will be more React projects coming soon"* - if it's not in the contract or a written email confirmation, it doesn't exist.

**Fix:** Ask to have it put in the offer letter or get email confirmation from HR or the hiring manager.

**Mistake 3 – Not checking Glassdoor or ITviec reviews:** 80% of red flags have already been documented by former employees.

**Fix:** Search "[company name] review ITviec" and read the 5–10 most recent reviews before your interview, not after.

**Mistake 4 – Being dazzled by brand name:** A big-name company doesn't automatically mean a good environment for juniors. Some large corporations slot entry-level hires into legacy maintenance teams with no clear growth path.

**Fix:** Ask the team lead directly about the junior roadmap - not HR.

**Mistake 5 – Rejecting without trying to negotiate first:** Many offers can be improved if you know how to push back. Declining without even attempting a counter-offer means leaving opportunity on the table.

**Fix:** Read [how to negotiate your first IT salary](/junior-developer-salary-negotiation) and try a counter-offer at least once before walking away for good.

## FAQ

**Q: Will rejecting an offer hurt my chances of applying to that company again in the future?**

Not if you decline politely and with a clear, professional reason. Experienced HR professionals understand that candidates have the right to choose. Send a thank-you email and keep the connection warm - the IT market is smaller than you think.

**Q: I already accepted, but I want to back out. Is that okay?**

Yes, but do it as soon as possible. Withdrawing an accepted offer isn't ideal, but it's still better than joining and quitting after one month. Send a professional, sincere apology email with a brief explanation.

**Q: How do I know if a salary offer is in line with the market?**

Check [entry-level and junior IT salary benchmarks for 2026](/entry-level-software-engineer-salary-2026) for a solid baseline. You can also look at ITviec, TopDev, and ask directly in IT community groups on Facebook or LinkedIn.

**Q: What if I only have one offer and I genuinely need the money right now?**

That's a tough spot. If you have to accept, set a personal deadline: *"I'll stay here for a maximum of 6 months while continuing to apply elsewhere."* Don't let short-term comfort turn into a long-term trap.

**Q: Do I need to give a reason when I reject an offer?**

You don't have to, but you should. Keeping it brief is fine: *"After careful consideration, I've decided to pursue a direction that better aligns with my current career goals."* No need to name another company or criticize this one.

**Q: Should I reject an offer by email or phone call?**

Email is the safer choice - it creates a written record, comes across as professional, and doesn't put either party on the spot. Send it within 24 hours of making your decision. Suggested subject line: *"Offer Response – [Your Name]"*.

## Conclusion

Rejecting an IT job offer isn't failure - it's a skill. A great fresher isn't someone who accepts every offer; it's someone who can tell the difference between an offer that helps them grow and one that just fills up time.

The 5 flags to remember: unjustified below-market salary, chaotic hiring process, vague job description, no mentor or onboarding plan, and pressure to sign on the spot.
