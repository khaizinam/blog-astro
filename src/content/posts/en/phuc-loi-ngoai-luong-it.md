Most IT freshers look at exactly one number when they receive an offer: the base salary. But **benefits beyond salary** are what create the real gap - two people earning the same 15M VND can have total compensation that differs by 30–40% depending on what's included. This article covers every benefit worth asking about in an IT interview, how to ask without seeming demanding, and the warning signs that a company is hiding something.

#### 1. Why benefits matter more than you think

Say you receive an offer of 15M VND gross. Company A offers nothing beyond that. Company B offers the same base salary but adds: private health insurance covering the whole family (ranging from 1.5–5M/month depending on the plan), 15 days of annual leave, remote work 3 days per week (saving roughly 1M/month in commuting costs), and a 5M VND annual learning budget.

Your real compensation from Company B is 4–5M per month higher than Company A - equivalent to a 25–30% salary increase - without negotiating a single extra dong in base pay.

This is why benefits aren't a side topic. They're a core part of any offer decision.

#### 2. Financial benefits

This group has the clearest monetary value and should be fully understood before signing anything.

**Bonuses:**
- Is there a 13th-month bonus? Are there conditions attached (must complete 1 full year, must not exceed X sick days)?
- Is there a performance bonus (KPI/OKR-based)? Quarterly or annual? How is it calculated?
- Are there project completion bonuses?

**Allowances:**
- Transportation allowance (fuel, parking)
- Meal allowance (or does the company have a cafeteria?)
- Phone and internet allowance (especially relevant for remote roles)
- Housing allowance (some companies working with overseas clients include this)

**Referral bonus:** Some companies pay when you successfully refer a hire. Not large, but worth knowing.

**Equity/ESOP:** Less common for freshers, but if you're joining a funded startup, ask about it - not to set expectations but to understand who you're actually working for and what upside exists.

#### 3. Health benefits

**Health insurance:** Statutory social health insurance contributions are legally required - but many companies purchase supplemental private health insurance (PVI, Bao Viet, AIA, etc.). Ask:
- Does the plan cover just the employee or the whole family?
- What's the annual maximum payout?
- Does it include dental and vision?

**Annual health checkup:** Does the company organize a yearly checkup? What does the checkup package include?

**Mental health support:** More tech companies now offer this - mental health days, counseling hotlines, access to therapy services. Asking about it signals maturity, not weakness.

#### 4. Time and flexibility benefits

This group directly affects quality of daily life - and for many people, it matters more than base salary.

**Annual leave:**
- How many days per year? (The legal minimum is [12 days under Vietnam's Labor Code 2019](https://thuvienphapluat.vn/van-ban/Lao-dong-Tien-luong/Bo-luat-lao-dong-2019-333670.aspx); good companies typically offer 15–18)
- Can unused leave be carried over to the next year?
- Do freshers in their first year get the full leave entitlement immediately, or only after completing one year?

**Flexible hours (flextime):**
- Is there a flexible start time? For example, can you choose to start anywhere between 8–10am?
- Can you leave early on days when your tasks are done?

**Remote and hybrid:**
- What is the current remote policy? Full remote, hybrid - how many days per week?
- Can this policy change? Who decides?
- If remote, does the company provide a home office setup allowance?

**Overtime:**
- How is overtime compensated - overtime pay rate or compensatory time off?
- Is overtime expected regularly or only in exceptional circumstances?

#### 5. Career development benefits

This is the group freshers most consistently overlook - and the one with the highest long-term value.

**Learning budget:**
- Does the company provide budget for courses, certifications, or books?
- What's the annual maximum per employee?
- Are there conditions attached (must stay for X years after using the budget, or must repay if you leave within X months)?

**Conference and workshop access:**
- Can you attend technical conferences? Who covers the cost?

**Promotion roadmap:**
- Is the path from fresher to junior to mid to senior clearly defined?
- How often is salary reviewed - twice a year or annually?
- What specific criteria determine a promotion?

**Mentoring:**
- Is there a formal mentoring program, or do you figure things out on your own?
- Who will be your technical mentor during the first three months?

#### 6. How to ask about benefits without losing points

Many freshers avoid asking about benefits because they think it will make them look greedy or unprofessional. In reality, asking the right questions at the right time signals that you understand your own value and know how to evaluate total compensation.

**Right timing:** After the recruiter or HR has mentioned the package, or when they ask "do you have any questions?" Don't raise benefits during a technical interview with an engineering manager.

**How to ask naturally:**

Instead of: *"What benefits does the company offer?"* (too generic, sounds like you're running through a checklist)

Ask in context:
- *"Beyond the base salary, could you walk me through the benefits package? I'm particularly interested in health insurance and leave policy."*
- *"I've been thinking about remote work flexibility - what does the current setup look like for this team?"*
- *"Does the company support learning budget or technical certifications? I'm currently working toward an AWS certification."*

See [Questions to Ask Your Interviewer as a Junior Dev](/en/cau-hoi-nguoc-phong-van) for more on how to frame benefit questions naturally within the conversation.

**Questions to avoid asking too early:**
- "Is the 13th-month bonus guaranteed?" - sounds like you're demanding a commitment before you've even started
- "If I leave early, how much do I keep?" - signals you're already planning an exit

These questions are appropriate once you've received an offer and are in formal negotiation. See [Salary Negotiation for Fresh IT Graduates](/en/dam-phan-luong-fresher-it) for how to structure that conversation properly.

#### 7. Red flags: when a company is hiding something

Not everything in an offer letter is transparent. Watch for these patterns:

**Vague benefit descriptions:** "The company offers many attractive benefits" without specifics - ask for a written breakdown. If they decline to put it in writing, that's a red flag.

**Bonus "depending on company performance":** This language commits to nothing. Ask directly: *"What was the average bonus for someone in a similar role last year?"*

**Large unexplained gap between gross and net:** If gross is 15M but net comes out to 10M, you need to understand exactly how that works before signing.

**No written remote policy:** If a company says "we're very flexible" but has no documented policy, you have no protection when a conflict arises.

**Evasiveness about salary reviews:** Good companies have a clear review schedule - every 6 months or annually. If HR says "it depends on performance" without giving a timeline, that's a transparency problem.

If you notice several of these patterns at once, read [When to Turn Down a Job Offer](/en/tu-choi-offer-it) for more perspective before making your decision.

#### 8. FAQ

**Should freshers ask about benefits during the interview?**
Yes, but timing matters. A technical interview round is not the place. The HR round or post-offer stage is appropriate. Asking about benefits doesn't make you look greedy - asking at the wrong moment does.

**If a company has few benefits, can I ask for higher salary instead?**
Absolutely, and this is smart negotiation. If private health insurance isn't included, you can say: *"I noticed the benefits package doesn't include supplemental health insurance. Would the company be open to adjusting the base salary so I can purchase a personal plan?"* Converting missing benefit value into a salary ask is completely legitimate.

**Is the learning budget actually used or does it just exist on paper?**
Depends entirely on the company culture. Ask directly: *"Could you give me an example of a course or certification someone on the current team has completed with company support recently?"* If they can't provide a concrete example, the policy likely exists only on paper.

**Does the difference between 12 and 15 days of leave actually matter?**
Three extra days per year sounds small, but it compounds over time - especially if unused leave can be carried over. More important than the raw number is whether unused leave is forfeited at year end. If it is, you're effectively losing money when you don't take it.

**Should I compare benefits across multiple offers?**
Yes, and this is the right way to think about it. Build a total compensation comparison table rather than comparing salaries alone - include estimated net salary, health insurance value, leave policy, expected bonus, and remote work value. The offer that comes out highest in total is actually the better one.

## Summary

Benefits beyond salary aren't optional extras - they're part of your total compensation that you have every right to ask about, negotiate, and walk away from if they don't meet your needs.

Quick checklist before signing any offer:

- [ ] 13th-month and performance bonus - conditions clearly stated?
- [ ] Private health insurance - what exactly does it cover?
- [ ] Annual leave - how many days, can it be carried over?
- [ ] Remote/hybrid - is there a written policy?
- [ ] Learning budget - evidence it's actually used?
- [ ] Salary review schedule - timeline and criteria clearly defined?
